Use of Flashbacks

March 23, 2020

Every chapter in the book, “Rocks fall, Everyone Dies” starts with a regular section, then there is a section that is before the events of the regular section. The one exemption to this is the final chapter where instead of back in time the book moves forward in time. This seems to tie into the fact that in the middle of the book, Aspen figures out that the woman who he thinks is his grandma is truly his great-great-great etc. grandma, she is immortal by stealing healt from other people and his real grandma died many years before. In this sense he is shown a whole new version of the past and that parallels the way the reader is constantly given parts of the past. The writing technique also helps build tension in the form of cliff hangers and make the epilogue of the book more meaningful.

The Craziness of plant domestication.

March 31, 2020

The book Guns, Germs, And Steel asks the question how did, often toxic, inedible, and small plants become domesticated long before humans had any idea about genetics and punnett squares (pg 114). The answer is so elegant and intuitive that it shocked me. He explained how plants were domesticated by humans in the most genitact way possible, survival of the fittest. For example most wild almonds are toxic and bad tasting, only 12 are needed to kid a would be farmer, but the toxicity is only controlled by one gene. If a hunter-gatherer eats a wild almond one day that has a mutation that makes it non-toxic, they would likely take more almonds from that bush back to their camp. Perhaps a few of those almonds are dropped near the camp and grow into new bushes. These non-toxic plants now have a competitive advantage over their toxic brothers and thus plants began domestication before humans began agraical.

Chapter 11, “The Lethal Gift of Livestock”, asks the question why were the spread of germs between the European and the Native Americans. The book remarks about how, “If recent reappraisals of the pre-Columbian New World population are correct, it was not far below the contemporary population of Eurasia. Some New World cities like Tenochtitlán were among the world’s most populous cities at the time. Why didn’t Tenochtitlán have awful germs waiting for the Spaniards?” One possible reason was the lack of domesticated animals in the New World. Even the animals domesticated in the Americas such as ducks and turkeys were not nearly as prone to giving humans diseases as cows and pigs. Another was the lack of connections between civilizations in the Americas. During the roman times, much of northern Africa, Europ, and Asia and this helped spread the Afro-Eursaiosn diseases. Erupe was home to diseases from all over the world but in the Americas even the massive Aztec Empire had almost no contact with the populations of the Mississippi valley chiefdoms. All of this reminded me of the Coronavirus spreading through the world now, since most counties have greatly limited travel and we can look at the modern differences in preventions between areas. Hyper dense hubs such as New York are being hit the hardest, but social distancing can artificially lower the interactions per square mile. As hunter gatherer societies had low population densities and low interactions per square mile they had a harder time fostering the creation of diseases than the high density connected European cities.
